Lecture 3: Software Myths, General Issues & Ethics

1. Software Myths

A Software Myth is a wrong belief about software and the process used to build it. These misunderstandings lead to poor decisions and project failures.

Management Myths

Myth 1: "We have a book of standards and procedures. Won't that give my people everything they need?"

Reality: The book may exist, but is it used? Is it current? Does it reflect modern practices? Is it complete and adaptable? A dusty standards document that nobody reads provides zero value.

💡 Example: Many companies have ISO documentation that was written in 2005 and never updated — developers don't even know it exists.

Myth 2: "If we fall behind schedule, we can add more programmers and catch up (The Mongolian Horde concept)."

Reality: Software development is not a mechanical manufacturing process. Adding new programmers to a late project makes it even later — new people need time to learn the codebase, and experienced developers must spend time training them instead of coding.

💡 Brook's Law: "Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later." — Fred Brooks, The Mythical Man-Month

Customer Myths

Myth 3: "A general statement of objectives is sufficient to begin writing programs — we can fill in details later."

Reality: An ambiguous "statement of objectives" is a recipe for disaster. Clear, unambiguous requirements are developed only through effective and continuous communication between customer and developer.

💡 Example: Client says "I want a fast website." Developer builds it on expensive servers. Client meant "fast to navigate" not "fast to load." Miscommunication wastes money.

Myth 4: "Software requirements continually change, but change can be easily accommodated because software is flexible."

Reality: While software requirements do change, the impact varies with timing: early changes (before design/coding) cost little, but late changes (after coding is complete) can cost 100x more.

Practitioner's Myths

Myth 5: "Once we write the program and get it to work, our job is done."

Reality: "The sooner you begin 'writing code,' the longer it'll take you to get done." Maintenance consumes 60-80% of the total software lifetime cost.

Myth 6: "Until I get the program 'running' I have no way of assessing its quality."

Reality: Technical reviews can be applied from the very beginning of a project to detect defects early. Code review, design review, and requirements review all improve quality before a single line runs.

💡 Example: Google conducts mandatory code reviews before merging any code to detect bugs at the cheapest possible stage.

Myth 7: "The only deliverable work product for a successful project is the working program."

Reality: A working program is only ONE part of a software configuration. A variety of work products — plans, documents, models, test cases — provide the foundation for successful project support.

2. General Issues Affecting Software

Three broad challenges affect all software projects today:

Issue 1: Heterogeneity

Systems must operate as distributed systems across networks that include different types of computers and mobile devices. New software must integrate with old legacy systems written in different programming languages.

Legacy System: Any outdated computing system (hardware or software) that is still in use.

Example: A modern React web app must connect to a 1990s COBOL banking backend. Bridging these systems is a heterogeneity challenge.

Issue 2: Business & Social Change

Businesses and society change quickly. Organizations need to replace old systems with updated ones, but traditional software engineering is too slow — delivery often takes longer than planned.

Example: COVID-19 forced companies to move to remote work overnight. Software teams that couldn't adapt quickly lost business.

Issue 3: Security & Trust

As software is intertwined with all aspects of our lives, we must ensure software is trustworthy and secure. Malicious users must not be able to attack our software or compromise information security.

Example: The 2017 WannaCry ransomware attack infected 200,000 computers in 150 countries because of poor security practices in software.

3. Software Engineering Ethics

Software engineers must behave in an honest and ethically responsible way. The key ethical responsibilities are:

1. Confidentiality

Respect the confidentiality of your employers or clients, irrespective of whether a formal agreement has been signed or not.

💡 Example: If you work on a client's new product idea, you must not share that idea with competitors even after leaving the company.

2. Competence

  • You should not misrepresent your level of competence.
  • You should not knowingly accept work that is outside your competence.
💡 Example: If a client asks you to build a nuclear reactor control system and you have no safety-critical systems experience, you must decline or bring in experts.

3. Intellectual Property Rights

  • Be aware of local laws governing intellectual property (patents, copyright).
  • Ensure the intellectual property of employers and clients is protected.

IPR (Intellectual Property Rights) are the rights given to persons over the creations of their minds.

4. Computer Misuse

Do not use technical skills to misuse other people's computers. Computer misuse ranges from trivial (playing games on employer's machine) to extremely serious (spreading viruses or malware).

💡 Example: Using your systems-admin access to read other employees' private emails is a clear ethical and legal violation.
